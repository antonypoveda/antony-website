## Finding useful things
Found and tested out a couple of tools that could prove useful for the day job.

[Mist](https://mist.inanimate.tech/) - created by [Matt Webb](https://interconnected.org/), Mist allows multiple people to work on the same markdown file, includes comments. 

Helpful for: avoiding trying to "collaborate" in Word.

[Twine](https://twinery.org/) - primarily designed for creating 'choose your own adventure'-style interactive fiction, Twine also work as a basic HTML prototype generator. You can copy in pages of markdown to quickly create a set of linked pages with very little technical knowledge. And because it's just a file, a user can open it easily in their browser for testing. 

We actually used Twine to sketch out our [interactive theatre shows](https://rising-ape.com/front-page/grid/presents/publish-or-perish/) for Rising Ape (see below) and I am made up to remember it and work out how useful it is for content design. I should probably write this up into its own post.

Helpful for: avoiding faffing in Figma and risking inaccessible prototypes

[NetNewsWire](https://netnewswire.com/) + [Feedly](https://feedly.com/i/my/me) - Yes, RSS feed readers. Rest assured, if you're writing interesting stuff, I am typing '/feed' in your sites URL and grabbing it.

Helpful for: avoiding "social" media.


## Sorting out an old website
I caught up with my pal [James Riley](https://www.linkedin.com/in/jamesrileyrac/) and we talked about what to do with the website for our old side hustle/creative outlet, [Rising Ape Collective](https://rising-ape.com).

We decided we're going to keep the site up, and make clear that the group is now longer active. The running costs are very low and we think it's still a useful record of what the people in Rising Ape achieved in the fields of science communication and public engagement with research. The live events in particular were so much fun to create, and you never know what ideas might be sparked by having them documented.

I contacted everyone we worked with to see if they were ok with keeping their name and role on the site. Obviously, it's important that people can control the info about themselves on the internet. It's also been lovely to hear back from everyone and see what they're up to. 

There's also been some cool comments on how getting involved with Rising Ape helped people's careers. We'd always thought of the group as a kind of 'scicomm accelerator' that gave people space to experiment and get experience in different parts of the field. It's super nice to hear this validated years later. And maybe one day, the Ape will rise again?
