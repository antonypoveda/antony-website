## Changing the garden
![Close up of small bright orange flowers on a sunny day. In the out of focus background are a couple of other flowers, pink and red.](/assets/images/blog/garden.jpg)

I've been shepherding a blog post, from the working group lead on the process to update [content designer](https://ddat-capability-framework.service.gov.uk/role/content-designer), through various approvals. Nearly there. 

There's a line in there about how updating the framework's content is 'not one person's job'. It requires a dedicated group to get involved and work out how best to change it.

Sometimes at work I speak about how [continuously improving](https://cddo.blog.gov.uk/2024/09/18/how-were-continuously-improving-the-digital-and-data-capability-framework/) the framework's content is like community gardening. Tending, observing, pruning, caring for something to keep it fresh over time. But what *kind* of garden are we looking after?

When the team started the work it's fair to say we had something like a once grand landscape garden of a country house, starting to grow wild or withering in places. People came to see it, but they noticed. Maybe people thought 'I can do better than this'. Maybe less people came.

So we brought in the community. We've said to groups 'You can dig in, plant new things, cut back what's no longer needed. We'll help, provide tools, and manage the overall landscaping, but which plants you use, and how you arrange them, is up to you.'

And now the garden is alive, changing, with groups busy all over the place, looking after their own patch.

And maybe that's good, it's valuable, it's enough? But there's part of me nagging 'Okay, more people come to the garden, but are they getting enough of what they need? Or do some still leave thinking "that's nice for you in your country house, but I'm still worried about sustaining my own little patch"'.

So what other kinds of garden are there?

Certainly we don't want some sort of Lancelot 'Capability' Brown (geddit?) to come in and impose a singular vision of the perfect Arcardian landscape. Still useful for inspiration perhaps, but totally physically disconnected from the gardens of Jo Public Servant. And again, risking neglect just a few years down the line unless heavily resourced.

So instead maybe we can *move* the garden, from the country estate into the neighbourhood itself. And make it a *kitchen* garden, where people can come and plant for their own nourishment, not just aesthetics. Where others can easily take what they need, for food or as seeds for their own patches. A self-sustaining place, where decisions about what is grown is totally down to the people using the garden, and where people contribute their own fruit and veg from home, inspiring others. Real food, for thought.

Of course, there's potential constraints and risks if we went for something like an 'open contribution framework'. But I'm not sure the current approach is any less risky. There's certainly appetite around the place for 'step changes, new paradigms' etc... I'll try out the metaphor and see if it helps people to think differently about ways to achieve our goals.

 It might not. I've also often described the framework content as 'tools to help make better decisions about skills', so maybe a 'community tool shed' may fit better? Let's see.
